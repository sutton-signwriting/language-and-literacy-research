# Reading Signed Text

**Recognition, syntax, and spatial reference in signed-language literacy**

This paper asks the next research-facing question after *The Four Language Pathways*:

> if signed language gains durable written form, what would reading mean in that system?

That question matters because a research series cannot stop at the claim that signed language can be written.

It has to say, more carefully, what becomes legible, what kinds of recognition problems emerge, how syntax and spatial reference might survive into text, and which questions remain open.

This paper does not pretend to resolve all of that.

Its narrower purpose is to define the reading problem clearly enough that the research space stops sounding vague.

## Terminology note

This paper uses *reading pathways* in a narrower sense than the developmental pathway model.

*The Four Language Pathways* used *pathways* for developmental routes into language and literacy.

This paper is narrower. It asks how signed text becomes readable:

- how written signs are perceived
- how they are recognized as units
- how they are compared, revisited, and parsed
- how they reach language interpretation during reading

## Why this paper comes next

*The Four Language Pathways* established a developmental distinction:

- language comes first
- literacy comes second
- literacy input and literacy output pathways matter

That model opens the research series.

The next step is to ask what the literacy layer would actually require once it exists.

For psycholinguistics, literacy research, and sign-language research, that means moving from:

- can signed language have durable written pathways?

to:

- what would readers do with those reading pathways once the literacy layer exists?

That is the problem of reading signed text.

## Reading is not only decoding

Many discussions of writing still begin from alphabetic decoding.

That is useful in some contexts, but it is too narrow as a general model of literacy.

Readers do more than convert one stream into another.

They:

- recognize recurring forms
- compare neighboring forms
- recover structure
- revisit earlier material
- build expectations across text
- use durable symbols as reusable objects

Those operations matter in any writing system.

The question here is how they would operate in a visual-spatial writing system for signed language.

That does not mean existing reading science is irrelevant.

It means the starting question has to be adjusted.

The `Simple View of Reading` helps distinguish text recognition from language comprehension. Dual-route and word-recognition models help explain how printed words may be processed in familiar writing systems. Eye-movement research gives tools for studying fixation, regressions, rereading, and layout effects.

Signed text can use those tools, but it cannot simply inherit all of their assumptions.

The unit being recognized may be a written sign rather than a printed word. Spatial composition may be part of the written form rather than surrounding layout. Reading may involve symbol recognition, signbox structure, viewpoint, and syntactic reference in ways that do not map cleanly onto alphabetic decoding.

## Recognition in signed text

The first reading question is recognition.

What would it mean to recognize a written sign?

A reader is not only following a sequence of letters.

A reader is encountering a bounded written object that preserves:

- symbol identity
- relative placement
- grouped composition
- authored viewpoint

That means recognition is not exhausted by symbol naming.

It involves recovering a composed sign as a unit.

This is one reason SignWriting should not be treated as merely an unusual display of decomposed features.

The written sign is not only a list.

It is a structured visual object.

That suggests at least three reading questions.

### 1. Unit recognition

How quickly and reliably do readers recognize a written sign as a whole?

### 2. Near-neighbor competition

How do visually similar written signs compete with one another during reading?

### 3. Internal structure

Which aspects of the sign are treated as most informative for fast recognition:

- handshape
- movement
- placement
- facial markers
- overall spatial composition

These are ordinary reading questions in a new writing environment.

## Reading and comparison

Durable text changes more than one-shot recognition.

It also changes comparison.

Readers can:

- compare one form with another
- compare one occurrence with another
- compare related signs across a lexicon or corpus
- revise and revisit the same written material

This matters because comparison is part of what makes literacy powerful.

Video can preserve performance well.

But writable text makes side-by-side inspection, repeated lookup, annotation, and structured reuse much easier to routinize.

So the reading problem is not only “can the text be read?”

It is also:

> what kinds of comparison become normal once signed language is available as durable text?

That is part of why written sign matters scientifically.

## Layout as a reading variable

Reading signed text is not only a question of symbol recognition inside an isolated sign.

Passage layout may also affect the reading pathway.

This is visible in the history of SignWriting layout.

Early text was written horizontally. Valerie Sutton later documented the vertical shift as a Deaf-user-driven change: Deaf staff members Lucinda O'Grady Batch, George "Butch" Zein, Kathy Say, Bonita Ewan, and Kevin Clark expressed interest in writing down the page in columns, and Sutton officially announced the switch to vertical writing for DAC publications in January 1997.

Sutton also described the same practical history on the SignWriting List in 2004: Deaf native-signing staff who had written SignWriting by hand in the 1980s told her they wanted to write vertically.

The historical point is therefore well documented.

The reading-speed point is more modest.

After the change, a small group of Deaf staff and readers reported that vertical writing felt easier and faster.

That history is not a large-scale experimental result. It is a small self-reported design observation from roughly five people who were directly affected by the change.

Even so, it matters for research framing.

It suggests that layout direction may change how readers maintain orientation to the signed body, left-right relations, and the composed space of the sign while moving through a passage. Vertical writing may reduce the need to reorient each sign as a separate object in a horizontal row.

The research point is modest:

> layout direction should be treated as a reading variable

That does not settle the processing question. It makes the question more precise.

Future studies should be able to compare horizontal and vertical passages using the same signed content, with measures such as reader preference, comprehension, rereading behavior, speed, and eye-movement patterns.

## Early study dimensions

The reading questions become more useful when they are tied to possible tasks and measures.

The following table is not a fixed protocol.

It is a first operational map for turning the reading agenda into studies.

Near-neighbor competition means that written signs sharing handshape, location, movement, or spatial-configuration features may compete during recognition in ways analogous to neighborhood effects in print reading.

| Research area | Early task | Possible measures |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Unit recognition | Identify isolated written signs under controlled variation | Accuracy, latency, confidence |
| Similarity and near-neighbor competition | Compare visually similar written signs | Confusion rates, similarity ratings, priming effects |
| Internal structure | Read partial, masked, or feature-altered written signs | Feature-deletion effects, recognition thresholds, error patterns |
| Layout direction | Read the same signed passage vertically and horizontally | Comprehension, reading time, rereading, eye movements, preference |
| Syntax and reference | Resolve written spatial reference in short passages | Interpretation accuracy, explanation quality, recall |
| Text reuse | Annotate, revise, or compare written signed texts | Revision quality, navigation behavior, annotation patterns |

## Syntax in written signed language

One of the hardest questions follows from signed-language structure itself:

written spoken languages often map temporal order into spatial order fairly directly, but signed languages may also use space itself for reference and structure.

That means syntax cannot be treated as an afterthought.

A serious research series has to ask:

- how much structure remains sequential in reading?
- how much structure is carried by the bounded written sign?
- how much structure is distributed across layout, grouping, or convention?

The key point is not that signed language lacks syntax.

The key point is that written signed language may not externalize syntax in exactly the same way alphabetic print often does.

Sign-language linguistics already treats spatial grammar, reference, classifier constructions, role shift, and viewpoint as structured linguistic phenomena; the reading question is how those structures are preserved, conventionalized, or redistributed when signing becomes durable text.

That is a research problem, not a reason to dismiss the writing.

## Spatial reference and anaphora

A related issue concerns spatial reference.

Signed languages often use space for:

- reference tracking
- role relations
- anaphora
- viewpoint structure

The question for literacy is not whether this disappears in writing.

The question is how it is:

- preserved
- normalized
- reduced
- made conventional
- or redistributed across other textual cues

This is exactly where reading research and writing-systems research need one another.

Some questions are about language processing.

Others are about representational convention.

The current research series should make both visible.

## What current evidence supports

Current evidence supports several starting claims:

- signed language can have durable written form
- SignWriting preserves meaningful written structure rather than only glossing spoken language
- small reader-reported design histories suggest that layout direction affects ease of reading
- real readers and institutions already exist, especially in Brazil
- current institutional case studies exist, including South Korea

The next research layer should measure:

- exactly which recognition mechanisms dominate skilled reading
- exactly how much vertical layout changes speed, comprehension, or processing load
- exactly how syntax and spatial reference are processed in all reading contexts
- exactly which text forms are easiest or hardest for different reader populations

The current task is to frame the research question well enough that those experiments become easier to design.

## A research program that follows from this

Once the reading problem is stated more clearly, the next studies become easier to imagine.

### 1. Lexical recognition studies

- sign identification under controlled variation
- similarity and confusion patterns
- repeated exposure and fluency effects

### 2. Short-passage reading studies

- comprehension of short written passages
- navigation and revisiting behavior
- annotation and recall patterns
- horizontal versus vertical passage comparison

### 3. Syntax and reference studies

- how readers resolve reference in written signed text
- how viewpoint and spatial conventions affect interpretation
- how layout interacts with clause or discourse structure

### 4. Corpus and materials work

- building readable text sets for experiments
- collecting normalized passages and metadata
- developing comparison tasks and reusable stimuli

These are not fantasy studies.

They become realistic because the platform has:

- real texts
- real users
- real empirical anchors
- and a growing technical base

## Why this paper matters

This paper matters because it narrows the research series to something more recognizable.

It says that the hard-science question is not:

- whether language can exist without sound

The harder scientific questions are:

- what recognition looks like in signed text
- what comparison and reuse look like in signed text
- how syntax and spatial reference survive in written form
- what new reading research becomes possible once the text exists

That is a more useful research center.

## Conclusion

Signed-language literacy is not only the existence of a writing system.

It is the emergence of a new reading problem.

That problem includes:

- recognition
- comparison
- syntax
- spatial reference
- durable textual reuse

Those questions are not yet fully answered.

But they are clear enough to define a real research program.

That is the contribution of this paper.

It turns a general literacy claim into a more precise reading agenda.

The paper's strongest claim is therefore not that SignWriting reading has already been explained.

It is that signed-language writing makes a new class of reading questions experimentally visible:

- how readers recognize written signs as composed units
- how they navigate spatially organized signs and passages
- how they use durable signed text for comparison and reuse
- how syntax and spatial reference are preserved, conventionalized, or redistributed in written form

Those are not side questions after adoption.

They are the research problem that adoption makes possible.

The strongest next move after this paper is *Research Program for Literacy Without Sound*, which asks how those questions can be studied with real texts, real users, and real infrastructure.
