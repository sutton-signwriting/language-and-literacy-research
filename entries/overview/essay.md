# Sutton SignWriting Language and Literacy Research

*A research-facing series*

This series develops the research questions opened by signed-language writing. It asks how accessible language pathways, readable signed text, and reusable written corpora can support stronger developmental, literacy, cognitive, and language research.

It establishes a research agenda and study-design surface; it does not report completed outcome studies.

It is for:

- psycholinguists
- cognitive scientists
- literacy researchers
- language researchers
- sign-language researchers with developmental or experimental interests

The central questions are:

- how language development depends on accessible pathways for input and expression
- how literacy functions as a second developmental layer rather than primary language creation
- how the four-pathway model should be stated, tested, and refined
- what signed-language writing changes for development, reading, recognition, syntax, and research design
- what early studies, materials, corpora, and reusable research assets should be built next

In this series, *pathway* is an access-and-development term. It does not mean that each pathway maps to a separate neural circuit.

The series also distinguishes two literacy questions that are often blurred:

- how Deaf signers develop literacy in surrounding written languages such as Portuguese, English, or Korean
- what changes when a signed language itself also has durable readable and writable text

Both questions matter. This series focuses mainly on the second while treating the first as essential rather than obsolete.

## Research Posture

The series meets research-facing expectations:

- engagement with language acquisition, sign-language acquisition, reading science, Deaf literacy, and corpus research
- explicit model statements and testable predictions where a paper proposes a model
- separation between conceptual claims, documented use, pilot observations, and controlled evidence
- ethics and governance language for work involving Deaf signers, learners, institutions, and reusable texts
- external-validity limits when Brazil-centered work is used as the strongest practical starting point

The series uses the public conceptual essays as an entry point, but it carries its own research apparatus.

## Reading Order

The clearest path through the series is:

1. *The Four Language Pathways*
2. *Reading Signed Text*
3. *Research Program for Literacy Without Sound*

## Entry Roles

**The Four Language Pathways**

- states the first model paper of the series
- distinguishes language pathways from literacy pathways
- gives a research-facing account of access, development, and signed-language writing

**Reading Signed Text**

- clarifies what reading can mean in a signed writing system
- develops research questions around recognition, syntax, spatial reference, and reusable text

**Research Program for Literacy Without Sound**

- turns the pathway model and reading agenda into study design
- defines early research areas around reading and literacy, Brazil-grounded empirical work, and reusable corpora and materials
- keeps infrastructure visible as research infrastructure without turning the research agenda into a stewardship brief

## Relationship To Other Series

- *Sutton SignWriting Foundations* provides the readable public conceptual opening.
- *Sutton SignWriting Evidence and Adoption* shows that the space is real enough to study responsibly.
- *Sutton SignWriting Infrastructure and Program* explains the stewardship structure needed for cumulative research.
- *Writing and Representation in Sutton SignWriting* provides the writing-systems theory behind durable signed text.
- *Formal SignWriting* provides the technical infrastructure for corpus search, grammar, encoding, rendering, and tokenization work.

The immediate reader path is *Language Without Sound*, *The Four Language Pathways*, *Reading Signed Text*, and *Research Program for Literacy Without Sound*.
