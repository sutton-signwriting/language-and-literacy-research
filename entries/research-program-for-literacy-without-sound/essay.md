# Research Program for Literacy Without Sound

**Study design, materials, corpora, and the next empirical steps for signed-language literacy**

This entry turns *Sutton SignWriting Language and Literacy Research* toward practical study design.

It follows:

1. *The Four Language Pathways*
2. *Reading Signed Text*

Those papers define the research problem.

This entry asks what can be studied next.

Its core claim is simple:

> signed-language literacy research needs real readers, real materials, reusable data, and disciplined early studies

Its job is to turn the research series into a program that can begin.

## Why This Program Belongs Here

This program is research-facing because its center is empirical study design.

It asks:

- what should be studied?
- who should be studied first?
- what materials are needed?
- what early studies are realistic?
- what reusable research assets should phase one produce?

Infrastructure still matters, but here it matters as research infrastructure.

The stewardship and institutional case belongs in *Sutton SignWriting Infrastructure and Program*.

This entry should connect to that stewardship series without becoming a stewardship brief itself.

## What This Program Is Not

The research program needs clear boundaries.

It is not:

- a generic comparison of all signed-language scripts
- a project organized around defeating another writing or notation system
- a promise that one study will settle signed-language literacy
- an argument that neuroscience should come first
- a substitute for the public foundations or evidence series

The stronger research path is narrower and more constructive:

- study SignWriting as readable and writable text
- use real materials and real reader populations
- build reusable corpora, tasks, and documentation
- make later literacy, corpus, cognition, and education studies easier

That is enough.

## Three Research Areas

The program has three mutually reinforcing work areas.

### 1. Reading And Literacy Studies

This work area asks what happens when signed language becomes durable readable and writable text.

The central questions include:

- how readers recognize written signs
- how readers experience comfort, comprehension, and effort
- how passage layout affects reading
- how written signs support annotation, revisiting, and revision
- how text use differs from relying on video alone
- what kinds of texts are easiest or hardest for different reader groups

This work area follows directly from *Reading Signed Text*.

It treats reading as a research problem rather than as a simple yes-or-no claim.

### 2. Brazil-Grounded Empirical Work

Brazil belongs inside the program design because it is the strongest currently documented empirical center for SignWriting.

It offers:

- documented institutional density
- repeated teaching practice
- written materials
- university and educational activity
- a deeper public research record than many English-language readers realize

That does not mean all SignWriting research must happen in Brazil.

It means Brazil is the strongest current place to begin if the program wants real settings, real materials, and realistic partnerships rather than hypothetical users.

That starting point has to be stated carefully.

Brazil-centered findings should not be generalized automatically to ASL, BSL, DGS, LSM, LSE, or other signed-language communities.

Different communities have different adoption histories, educational systems, language ecologies, writing practices, technology access, and institutional relationships to SignWriting.

So Brazil is the strongest first site because it makes early research feasible.

It is not the whole validity argument.

Early Brazil-grounded work could include:

- updated institutional mapping
- materials collection
- teacher and reader interviews
- classroom-material review
- pilot studies with existing readers and learners
- documentation of current practices and constraints

### 3. Corpus, Materials, And Reusable Research Infrastructure

This work area makes the science cumulative.

It asks:

- what texts should be collected?
- what metadata should be recorded?
- what corpora can be shared responsibly?
- what short passages and isolated signs can become reusable stimuli?
- what annotation and search tools are needed?
- what documentation lets later researchers understand and reuse the material?

For the technical infrastructure behind corpus search, grammar, encoding, rendering, and tokenization, this strand should connect directly to the *Formal SignWriting* series.

Without this research-infrastructure strand, each study risks becoming isolated.

With it, later studies can compare results, reuse materials, refine tasks, and build a stronger field.

## Realistic Early Studies

The first phase should be modest and useful.

It does not need to begin with expensive instrumentation or sweeping outcome claims.

The strongest early studies are practical studies that produce reusable outputs.

### Institutional Mapping

Update the Brazil accounting.

Identify active institutions, teachers, learner populations, written materials, and current practices.

This gives later research a clearer empirical map.

### Materials Audit

Collect and describe written artifacts already in use:

- classroom materials
- books and literature
- short texts
- dictionaries
- worksheets
- online course materials

The goal is not only collection.

The goal is to understand what kinds of text already exist and which materials could support future studies.

### Reader-Experience Study

Study how Deaf SignWriting readers describe:

- comfort
- comprehension
- effort
- navigation
- annotation
- cultural identification
- preferred text forms and layouts

This kind of work should be treated carefully.

Self-report is not the same as controlled processing evidence, but it is a serious starting point for understanding reader experience and designing better studies.

### Short-Text And Task Pilots

Design small studies around:

- isolated written signs
- short passages
- instructional texts
- narrative texts
- horizontal and vertical passage layouts

The goal is to learn which stimuli, tasks, and measures are usable before making larger claims.

Possible measures include:

- preference
- comprehension
- recognition
- rereading behavior
- annotation behavior
- task completion
- recall

### Corpus-Building Pilot

Assemble a reusable written-Libras pilot corpus.

A useful pilot corpus could include:

- normalized text
- source context
- metadata
- basic annotation
- rights and reuse notes
- documentation of how the corpus was assembled

This would give later literacy, search, NLP, and corpus work a stronger base.

## Who And What Should Be Studied First

The strongest early populations are already visible if the program stays focused.

Useful early populations include:

- Deaf signers with early sign-language access
- current SignWriting learners
- SignWriting teachers and course designers
- Deaf academic readers where suitable

Useful early materials include:

- classroom texts
- books and literature
- dictionary and lexical materials
- short curricular passages
- instructional materials
- existing public or permission-cleared SignWriting texts

This combination keeps the work anchored in people and materials that already exist.

## Research Ethics And Community Governance

A research program with Deaf signers, learners, teachers, institutions, and written signed-language corpora needs ethical structure from the beginning.

Ethics cannot be reduced to a later consent form.

The program should be built around several commitments.

### Community involvement in study design

Research questions should be shaped with Deaf educators, Deaf readers, SignWriting users, and local institutional partners.

A study that treats Deaf participants only as data sources is weaker scientifically and ethically.

The strongest version of this program uses community knowledge to decide:

- which questions matter
- which texts are appropriate
- which tasks are natural or artificial
- which outcomes are useful to the community
- how findings should be returned and explained

### Accessible consent and participant protection

Consent materials should be accessible in the languages and modalities participants actually use.

For Deaf signers, that may require signed explanations, written summaries, interpreter support, and time for questions.

For learners, minors, and classroom studies, the program must distinguish:

- institutional permission
- teacher cooperation
- guardian consent where needed
- participant assent
- the learner's right not to participate

Participation should never be confused with normal classroom compliance.

### Data sovereignty, rights, and reuse

Reusable corpora are valuable, but they carry obligations.

Written signed-language texts may be public, institutional, educational, personal, literary, or community-owned. A corpus plan must say what may be shared, what may be quoted, what may be searched, what may be redistributed, and what must remain restricted.

Reuse should therefore be tiered:

- public-domain or openly licensed texts
- permission-cleared educational materials
- restricted research materials
- non-shareable materials used only for local analysis

Metadata should preserve source context, permissions, language, institution, text type, and reuse limits.

### Researcher and community interests

The program should distinguish researcher goals from community benefit.

Researchers may want publications, datasets, models, or grant outputs.

Communities may want better materials, teacher support, accessible findings, improved tools, or institutional recognition.

Good study design should make those interests explicit and avoid treating community benefit as an afterthought.

### Ethics review is necessary but not sufficient

Institutional review board approval or equivalent ethics review is necessary for human-subjects work.

It is not sufficient by itself.

The program also needs local governance, accessible communication, community review where appropriate, and clear plans for returning results.

## Why Brazil Matters Inside The Program

Brazil is not only background evidence.

It changes the feasibility of the research program.

The Brazilian record:

- reduces dependence on hypothetical users
- offers a stronger starting point for partnerships
- gives access to written materials and teaching practice
- makes cross-site and longitudinal work more imaginable
- supports a stronger grant and collaboration narrative

That is why Brazil belongs inside the program design rather than only in the evidence brief.

## External Validity And Replication

The first phase should treat Brazil as a strong empirical base and a limited generalization base.

Early studies should ask:

- what is true in the Brazilian settings studied?
- which tasks and measures work with existing readers and materials?
- which patterns look stable enough to test elsewhere?

Those studies prepare later claims about:

- whether Brazilian reader experience predicts patterns elsewhere
- how Libras materials compare with materials in other signed languages
- how one institutional ecosystem informs the wider literacy question

Later phases should therefore include cross-context replication:

- other signed languages
- different age groups
- different educational settings
- different levels of SignWriting exposure
- different layout and text genres

That sequence makes the Brazil-first strategy scientifically useful beyond one setting.

## Phase-One Deliverables Checklist

Phase one succeeds if it produces durable research assets.

That means:

- [ ] an updated institutional map
- [ ] a curated materials set
- [ ] a reusable pilot corpus
- [ ] early reader-experience findings
- [ ] tested short-text tasks
- [ ] clearer later-stage literacy and cognition questions
- [ ] a stronger basis for grant proposals and ethics-ready study design

These deliverables make later work stronger, cheaper, and more cumulative.

## Relationship To Infrastructure

Infrastructure is not separate from the science.

It affects what can be studied and whether later researchers can build on earlier work.

Research infrastructure includes:

- text collection
- normalization
- metadata
- annotation
- search
- preservation
- rights documentation
- reproducible stimuli and tasks

Those needs connect this entry to *Sutton SignWriting Infrastructure and Program*.

They also connect to *Formal SignWriting* wherever research corpora need stable encoded text, searchable forms, reproducible rendering, and task-specific tokenization.

But the research program should remain research-facing.

Its job is to define what should be studied and what reusable assets the studies should produce.

The infrastructure series explains the stewardship model that can make that work durable.

## Good-Idea Test

This program is strong if it remains:

- SignWriting-centered
- empirically grounded
- focused enough to begin
- reusable in its outputs
- clear about what evidence establishes and what the next study should test

It becomes weaker if it drifts into:

- comparison for its own sake
- abstract theory without studies
- oversized promises before materials and partnerships exist
- support rhetoric without research design

## Bottom Line

The most useful next move is not a bigger theory.

It is a concrete SignWriting-centered research program that starts with real readers, real materials, and reusable research infrastructure.

That program can begin modestly.

If it is designed well, it can grow into stronger literacy, corpus, cognition, education, and infrastructure work over time.
