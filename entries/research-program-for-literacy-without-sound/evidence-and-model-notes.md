# Evidence and Model Notes for Literacy Without Sound

**Brain networks, pathway expansion, literacy, and the case for SignWriting infrastructure**

These notes carry the heavier research model, comparative cases, and evidence questions behind the *Language Without Sound* argument.

They support the broader *Research Program for Literacy Without Sound* by gathering the conceptual evidence base behind language pathways, literacy pathways, signed-language writing, and research infrastructure.

They identify:

- what is firmly established
- what follows from that science
- where the structural gap lies for signed languages
- what SignWriting already demonstrates
- where the evidence remains promising but incomplete

They also gather the core references behind the argument in one place.

They function as research-program notes. They remain connected to *Sutton SignWriting Foundations: Language Without Sound*, but they are intentionally more detailed than a foundations companion note and should not be treated as a complete evidence review of signed-language literacy outcomes.

This is an evidence register and model notebook, not a single linear article.

It is meant to gather claims, distinctions, comparative cases, and source anchors that support the research program. Readers should use it as a structured support document rather than as a finished standalone review paper.

## How To Use These Notes

These notes serve three related purposes. Sections I-V gather the evidence status behind the pathway and literacy claims. Sections VI-VIII compare representation layers and clarify what different media can and cannot support. Sections IX-XI turn those limits into research questions, working formulations, and claim boundaries.

Readers checking evidence can begin with Sections I-V. Readers evaluating the comparative model can begin with Sections VI-VIII. Readers designing studies or citing the research agenda can begin with Sections IX-XI.

## I. What is strongly established

### 1. Language is not tied to sound

**Strength: Strong**

The broad scientific claim underlying the project is well supported.

The relevant findings include:

- sign languages are established natural languages
- signed languages recruit core language networks in the brain
- phonology is not limited to sound
- early access to natural language matters whether the language is signed or spoken
- reading and language processing can be studied in both modality-general and modality-specific ways

The strongest formulation here is:

> human language depends on accessible linguistic input, not on one privileged sensory channel

This does not mean that actual language use is unembodied or that modality is irrelevant.

It means that human language is not reducible to sound, and that core linguistic organization can be reached through different embodied modalities.

### 2. Language development and literacy development are not the same event

**Strength: Strong**

The evidence strongly supports a developmental distinction between:

- early language acquisition
- later literacy acquisition

Language must first reach the learner in an accessible form.

Literacy then expands that language system by adding durable symbolic routes for reading and writing.

The strongest supported statement here is:

> literacy builds on language; it does not substitute for missing language access

### 3. Literacy is a learned symbolic expansion

**Strength: Strong**

The literacy literature supports the following claims:

- reading is learned rather than innate
- print reading recruits and reorganizes existing perception-language systems
- Braille demonstrates that literacy can be built through touch
- durable symbol systems support revision, comparison, reuse, and accumulation in ways recordings alone do not

This matters because the argument about SignWriting is not only about representation.

It is about what becomes possible when a language acquires durable symbolic pathways.

## II. What follows from that science

If the previous claims are accepted, several consequences follow.

### 1. Pathway type matters more than modality prestige

The scientifically useful distinction is not between:

- spoken language as normal
- all other forms as deviations

The better distinction is between pathway types:

- language input pathways
- language output pathways
- literacy input pathways
- literacy output pathways

This framework lets spoken, signed, and tactile cases be compared without presupposing that speech is the standard against which all else must be measured.

### 2. Braille proves modality flexibility at the literacy level

Braille is especially important in this project because it demonstrates two things at once:

- literacy does not require vision
- durable symbolic systems reorganize what the brain can do with language

Braille should still be described carefully.

It is typically a tactile writing system rather than a separate language.

But it remains one of the strongest demonstrations that literacy can be built through more than one sensory route.

### 3. Writing changes use, organization, and scalability

The literature on reading, writing, corpora, annotation, and computation all point in the same direction:

- writable systems support repeated revision
- writable systems support comparison across time and speakers
- writable systems support search, reuse, and accumulation
- writable systems support structured data for science and technology

This bridge from literacy to infrastructure matters because the same symbolic properties that support reading and writing also support corpora, annotation, search, comparison, and computation.

Reading-affordances research helps sharpen this claim.

The strongest additional point is not that one medium is always better than another.

It is that reading depends on a bundle of affordances such as:

- navigation
- revisiting
- pacing
- annotation
- physical or spatial orientation within the text

Those affordances shape how readers compare passages, build cognitive maps of a document, and return to earlier material.

That matters here because the argument for writable sign is not only that a sign language can be represented symbolically.

It is that durable text changes the kinds of intellectual work that become routine.

So the careful formulation is:

> literacy depends not only on symbol access, but also on the affordances that support navigation, annotation, revisiting, and structured reuse

This strengthens the case for SignWriting infrastructure without forcing a simplistic claim that paper is always superior to screens or that materiality alone explains reading outcomes.

## III. Where the structural gap lies

### 1. Signed languages are fully linguistic but often lack equally mature written pathways

The clearest formulation is:

> signed languages have often lacked widely adopted, durable, native-facing written infrastructure comparable to the writing systems available for many spoken languages

### 2. Video is essential, but it is not the same as writing

**Strength: Conceptually strong**

Video is indispensable for signed languages.

It preserves performance, timing, movement, and expression extremely well.

But it remains time-based and linear.

That makes it much weaker than writable text for tasks such as:

- close editing
- repeated comparison
- searching
- structured annotation
- routine revision
- everyday literacy practice

The clearest formulation is:

> video preserves language well, but it does not itself create a direct read-write symbolic pathway comparable to literacy

### 3. Research transcription and everyday writing are not the same

Some sign-language notation and transcription systems are excellent for linguistic description and researcher-facing analysis.

That is a genuine scholarly strength.

But many such systems were not designed to function as ordinary reading and writing systems for routine native use.

The distinction is therefore:

- transcription can support analysis
- writing systems can support literacy, text production, and structured reuse

This distinction matters because the present argument concerns not only analysis, but also durable reading and writing pathways.

## IV. What SignWriting already demonstrates

### 1. SignWriting is a sustained object of research

**Strength: Strong**

Support includes:

- dedicated research archive
- dedicated publications archive
- historical dissertation and thesis lists
- symposium archives spanning multiple years and countries
- international dissertation and thesis production across multiple languages and institutions

Core links:

- [SignWriting research library](https://m.signwriting.org/library-research.html)
- [SignWriting research publications archive](https://m.signwriting.org/library-research-publications.html)
- [SignWriting dissertations and theses archive](https://www.signwriting.org/library/history/publications/dissertations.html)
- [SignWriting symposium archive](https://m.signwriting.org/library-symposium.html)

Together, these materials show durable scholarly presence across countries, languages, and institutional settings.

### 2. SignWriting already supports writing-system, software, and standards work

**Strength: Strong**

Support includes:

- comparative work on sign-language scripts
- Unicode proposal and follow-on technical work
- digital editor and tool development
- corpus construction
- sign-language processing work
- machine translation using Formal SignWriting representations
- evaluation work designed for SignWriting strings

This is one of the strongest parts of the case for SignWriting as infrastructure rather than only pedagogy.

### 3. SignWriting already supports a plausible infrastructure layer

The best-supported current claims are infrastructural rather than population-scale educational.

SignWriting already meets many of the criteria of infrastructure, even without formal institutional stewardship.

SignWriting already provides, or has already supported:

- readable and writable sign text
- structured and searchable representations
- corpora and lexicons
- software tooling
- computational representation
- standards-oriented technical work

That is enough to justify describing it as infrastructure in practice, not only as a notation proposal.

## V. What remains promising, but still limited

### 1. Educational outcomes

**Strength: Moderate for feasibility and use; limited for large-scale outcome claims**

The literature supports:

- feasibility
- actual use
- pedagogical relevance
- classroom experimentation
- documented educational use and promise of benefit

But the current record is still limited by:

- small samples
- case-based designs
- uneven outcome measures
- limited large controlled studies

The disciplined formulation remains:

> the literature supports feasibility, use, and scientific relevance more strongly than broad claims of large-scale literacy superiority

### 2. Neuroscience of written signed language

**Strength: Emerging**

The broader neuroscience base for signed language is strong.

The broader neuroscience base for reading and Braille is strong.

The specific neuroscience of reading written sign systems is still developing.

This is an opportunity, not a weakness in the overall framework.

It means the strongest current argument is:

- signed language is fully language
- literacy is a real second developmental layer
- SignWriting creates a plausible written pathway worth studying directly

### 3. Tactile integration

Touch belongs in the argument, but with precision.

- tactile signing is a language-access pathway
- Braille is typically a tactile writing system
- DeafBlind tactile language and literacy routes are real, but more variable and less fully mapped than the strongest spoken, signed, and Braille baselines

The strongest scientifically supported statement is:

> human language pathways can be reached through audition, vision, and touch

More specific claims should be framed carefully:

- it is well supported that blind Braille readers recruit touch-based and occipital reading networks
- it is well supported that deaf signers recruit core language networks through vision
- it is promising but less fully mapped how DeafBlind tactile language users recruit language networks across different tactile systems
- it is too strong to say that skilled reading simply eliminates phonological mediation; the safer claim is that skilled reading can become less dependent on overt phonological mediation

## VI. Comparative cases and what they show

The case structure is selective rather than exhaustive.

### 1. Hearing child with spoken language and print literacy

**What it shows**

- language develops first
- print adds durable literacy later

**Evidence status**

**Strongly supported**

### 2. Deaf child with early sign language

**What it shows**

- language does not depend on sound
- full language can develop through vision

**Evidence status**

**Strongly supported**

### 3. Blind child with spoken language and Braille literacy

**What it shows**

- literacy does not depend on vision
- durable symbolic pathways can be tactile

**Evidence status**

**Strongly supported**

### 4. Deaf child with early sign language plus a written sign pathway

**What it shows**

- the question is no longer only language access
- the question becomes whether signed language can gain its own durable reading and writing pathways

**Evidence status**

**Conceptually strong; large-scale outcome evidence still limited**

### 5. Language present in the environment, but not fully accessible

**What it shows**

- social availability is not the same as perceptual access
- later literacy cannot simply repair missing early language access

**Evidence status**

**Strongly supported for deprivation risk**

### 6. Supporting emergence cases

Homesign and Nicaraguan Sign Language remain useful supporting comparisons.

They show:

- resilience under constraint
- pressure toward language-making
- the difference between individual communicative invention and shared community language growth

They are best treated as supporting comparisons rather than central literacy cases.

## VII. Comparative models for signed language literacy and infrastructure

### Why this comparison is necessary

The argument of this project is that signed languages benefit from durable readable and writable pathways.

That claim raises an immediate and reasonable question:

> do existing models such as video, bilingual literacy, and transcription already provide what is needed?

To answer that question clearly, it is useful to compare the main models currently in use.

The goal of this section is not to rank them in general.

The goal is to clarify:

- what each model does well
- what each model does not attempt to do
- which layers of language use and infrastructure remain unaddressed

### The four dominant models

A useful working distinction is between four broad models:

- **video-centered ecosystems**
- **bilingual literacy** in a signed language plus a dominant written language
- **gloss and transcription systems**
- **native sign writing systems**, including Sutton SignWriting

Each of these should be understood as solving a different part of the problem.

A compact summary is:

- video -> performance and communication
- bilingual print -> societal literacy and access
- transcription -> analysis and research
- native sign writing -> direct writing within the language

### Model A. Video-centered ecosystems

**Primary role**: performance, communication, and documentation

Video is indispensable for signed languages.

It preserves:

- movement
- timing
- facial expression
- embodied performance

It is widely accessible and does not require learning a writing system.

However, video is time-based rather than symbol-based.

This makes it much weaker for:

- precise editing
- structured comparison
- searching within content
- routine annotation
- cumulative text-based work

The strongest formulation is:

> video preserves language extremely well, but it does not itself create a read-write symbolic pathway comparable to literacy

### Model B. Bilingual literacy

**Primary role**: societal literacy and institutional access

This model combines:

- signed language for communication and instruction
- a surrounding written language such as English or Portuguese for literacy

Its strengths are clear:

- access to established writing systems
- integration into education, publishing, and employment
- strong institutional stewardship

Its limitation is structural:

- the written form is not native to the signed language itself
- writing requires cross-language mapping
- direct written expression within the signed language remains limited

This model provides literacy.

It does not provide a **native-facing written pathway for the signed language itself**.

### Model C. Gloss and transcription systems

**Primary role**: analysis, annotation, and linguistic research

Systems such as gloss and HamNoSys are highly valuable for:

- linguistic description
- cross-language comparison
- structured annotation

They are strongest in controlled research contexts.

However:

- they are not designed for everyday reading or writing
- they are often difficult for non-specialists to use
- they do not function as general-purpose literacy systems

The key distinction is:

> transcription supports analysis; it does not replace writing as a literacy system

### Model D. Native sign writing systems

**Primary role**: direct reading and writing within signed language

Native sign writing systems attempt to provide:

- readable signed-language text
- writable signed-language text
- structured symbolic representation of sign

Within this category, different conceptions exist, including ASLwrite and SignWriting.

For this project, the focus is on **Sutton SignWriting**.

SignWriting is best described here in terms of its contribution:

- it provides a plane-based writing system for signed language
- it supports structured, searchable text
- it is linked to encoding through FSW and SWU and to software tools
- it supports corpora, datasets, and computational use

The most careful formulation is:

> Sutton SignWriting is currently the most developed and best-documented system in this project for adding a durable read-write pathway within signed language

### Comparative matrix (heuristic)

The table below summarizes the functional differences.

These are **comparative judgments for this argument**, not universal rankings across all contexts.

| Capability | Video | Bilingual Print | Transcription | SignWriting |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Preserve performance | strong | weak | weak | moderate |
| Native language expression | strong | limited | limited | strong |
| Editing and revision | weak | strong | strong | strong |
| Search and reuse | limited | strong | strong | strong |
| Everyday writing | weak | strong | weak | documented but not broadly institutionalized |
| Corpus and dataset use | weak | strong (for spoken language) | moderate | strong (for signed language) |

### Key point: different models, different layers

These models should not be treated as direct replacements for one another.

They operate at different layers:

- video -> preserves language
- bilingual print -> connects to broader literacy systems
- transcription -> enables analysis
- sign writing -> enables writable language within the modality

The central gap identified in this project is not the absence of language, or even the absence of literacy in general.

It is:

> the absence of widely adopted, durable, native-facing written pathways for signed language

### Infrastructural contribution

The role of SignWriting in this comparison is not to replace other models.

It is to contribute a layer that they do not provide:

- editable symbolic text within the language
- structured representations for corpora and datasets
- reusable forms for research and computation

This is best understood as an **infrastructural contribution**, not a universal solution.

### Open comparative questions

This comparison clarifies the next research questions:

- when does written sign improve literacy outcomes relative to bilingual + video models?
- which tasks benefit most from writable sign text?
- what are the costs of adoption in real educational settings?
- how do users move between video, written sign, and dominant-language text?

These questions remain open and define the next phase of study.

### Working conclusion

No single model fully supports all functions of signed language use.

- video is unmatched for performance
- bilingual print is unmatched for institutional literacy
- transcription is unmatched for analysis

But none of these alone provide:

> a native, durable, editable, and computationally tractable written form of signed language

That is the layer this project identifies.

## VIII. Relationship between ecosystem models and script systems

The previous section compared different **ecosystem models** for signed language use.

This section acknowledges that **writing systems themselves also differ in conception and purpose**.

These two levels should not be confused.

### Two different layers of comparison

It is useful to distinguish:

- **ecosystem models** -> how language is used, stored, and transmitted
- **script systems** -> how language is represented when written

These are related, but not identical.

A single ecosystem may include multiple script systems, and a script system may serve different roles across ecosystems.

### How script systems map onto ecosystem roles

#### Video-centered ecosystems

- do not depend on a writing system
- may use transcription such as HamNoSys for annotation
- rarely support everyday writing within the language itself

Script role: **optional and analytic**

#### Bilingual print ecosystems

- rely on dominant-language writing systems
- may incorporate gloss or alphabetic approximations such as ASL-phabet
- support literacy through another language rather than direct writing within the signed language

Script role: **bridge to another language**

#### Research and annotation ecosystems

- depend heavily on transcription systems such as HamNoSys
- are optimized for precision and cross-linguistic comparison

Script role: **analytic representation**

#### Native sign writing ecosystems

- aim to support direct reading and writing within signed language
- support structured reuse within the language itself

Within this category, different script conceptions exist:

- **ASLwrite** -> spatial script with community-driven development
- **SignWriting** -> spatial script linked to a stable symbol inventory, encoding through FSW and SWU, software tools, and growing standards alignment

Script role: **native literacy plus infrastructure**

### Key distinction

The central distinction is not:

> which script is better

It is:

> which systems support which functions

- alphabetic systems support **instructional mapping**
- transcription systems support **analysis**
- spatial scripts support **direct representation of signed language structure**
- infrastructure systems support **long-term storage, computation, and reuse**

These roles can overlap, but they are not interchangeable.

For a more focused comparison of ASL-phabet, HamNoSys, ASLwrite, and SignWriting, see **Other Sign Language Scripts and Their Conceptions**.

### Why this matters

This layered view prevents several common errors:

- treating all sign writing systems as doing the same job
- assuming that transcription systems can replace writing systems
- assuming that educational alphabets scale into infrastructure
- ignoring the difference between script design and system-level support

It also clarifies the central claim of this project:

> SignWriting should be evaluated not only as a script, but as a system that spans writing, encoding, software, datasets, and long-term infrastructure

The strongest current evidence supports feasibility, use, and infrastructure relevance.

Comparative outcome superiority across large-scale educational settings remains an open question.

## IX. What becomes possible once signed language is writable

If signed language gains durable readable and writable pathways, then the following become much more tractable:

### Cognition and neuroscience

- direct study of visual-spatial reading
- comparison of visual, tactile, and spoken literacy pathways
- experimental work using controlled written stimuli

### Literacy and education

- reading and writing instruction within signed language
- bilingual education questions
- durable teaching materials
- better comparisons between different literacy routes

### Linguistics and language documentation

- textual comparison
- lexicon building
- annotation systems
- corpora and archival continuity

### AI and language technology

- machine-readable sign representations
- datasets and evaluation methods
- translation and language-modeling pipelines
- long-term interoperability through standards

The argument therefore does not stop at orthography.

It extends toward infrastructure.

## X. Working formulations

The strongest framing lines remain:

- "The brain is waiting for accessible language, not for one privileged sensory channel."
- "Language comes first; literacy expands it."
- "Braille shows that literacy can become tactile."
- "Video preserves language well, but it does not itself create a direct read-write symbolic pathway."
- "SignWriting matters because it can add readable and writable pathways for signed language."
- "The literature supports feasibility, use, and scientific relevance more strongly than broad claims of large-scale literacy superiority."
- "Video preserves performance; bilingual print supports institutional literacy; transcription supports analysis; sign writing supports writable language within the modality."

## XI. Open research questions

These questions are especially useful for future-facing framing:

- How does adding a readable and writable sign pathway change literacy development for signers?
- What is the relationship between written sign representation and reading development?
- How do tactile writing and tactile signing compare with visual sign reading at the neural and behavioral levels?
- How can SignWriting be used as an input, output, or intermediate representation in NLP systems?
- What shared infrastructure would most effectively support corpora, lexicons, annotation, and standards work?
- How might written sign systems interact with modality-specific and modality-general language pathways in the brain?
- What written supports are most effective for DeafBlind learners across tactile and mixed-modality environments?
- What kinds of datasets and evaluation metrics best capture visual-spatial language structure in SignWriting representations?
- How can formal specifications such as FSW and SWU best support interoperability, archival use, and long-term computational work?
