# The Four Language Pathways

**A developmental model of language, literacy, and signed-language writing**

This paper carries the opening model claim for *Sutton SignWriting Language and Literacy Research*: signed-language writing should be studied through distinct pathways for language input, language output, literacy input, and literacy output.

The model separates language development from literacy development. It treats accessible input and expressive output as developmental necessities, and it treats literacy as a durable symbolic layer that can be added after language begins.

For signed languages, the research question is not only whether signing is language. It is what changes when a signed language also has durable readable and writable text.

This paper turns the public foundations argument in *Language Without Sound* into a research-facing model. It defines the four pathways, explains why the distinctions matter, and identifies the research space opened by signed-language writing.

## Terminology note

This paper uses *developmental pathways* in a broad sense.

It is about routes by which language and literacy become available to a learner and user:

- language input
- language output
- literacy input
- literacy output

It is not yet about the narrower reading-pathway question of how written signs move from page to eye to perception to language interpretation during skilled reading.

When this paper says *pathways* without qualification, it means developmental pathways unless the context says otherwise.

It also does not mean that each pathway maps to a discrete neural circuit.

Here, *pathway* is an access-and-development term: it names how language or literacy becomes available for use, not a completed neurocognitive model.

## Problem statement

Current discussion of signed-language writing often still blurs three different things:

- language as a developmental system
- literacy as a later symbolic layer
- modality as the route through which access happens

That blur creates unnecessary confusion.

If language is treated as sound, then signed language has to begin by defending its status as language.

If literacy is treated as equivalent to language, then early language access and later symbolic access are hard to separate.

If modality is treated as destiny, then questions about accessibility, deprivation, literacy, and writing are reduced to preference for one channel rather than analyzed as developmental pathway questions.

The four-pathway model separates those issues while preserving the importance of modality.

Its claim is narrower:

> what matters developmentally is whether accessible pathways exist for language input, language output, literacy input, and literacy output

That shift allows a clearer account of what comes first, what comes second, and what signed-language writing changes.

This paper also distinguishes literacy for Deaf signers from literacy within signed language.

Deaf signers may and often must develop literacy in surrounding written languages such as Portuguese, English, Korean, or other national and regional written languages.

That remains essential.

The present model asks a different question:

> what changes when the signed language itself also has durable readable and writable text?

## Relation to existing research

Input, output, language, and literacy are not new ideas by themselves.

Language acquisition research has long shown that timing and accessibility of language input matter. Sign-language research has shown that signed languages are full natural languages and that delayed first-language access can have lasting consequences. Reading research has long distinguished language comprehension, decoding or recognition, and broader comprehension processes.

This paper uses those established findings for a narrower purpose.

It asks how the pieces should be reorganized when the language in question is signed and when the proposed literacy layer is not print for a surrounding spoken language, but durable written signed language.

That makes the model different from several familiar frameworks:

- The `Simple View of Reading` is useful for separating linguistic comprehension from text recognition, but it was built around print-reading questions and does not by itself ask whether a signed language has direct literacy input and output pathways of its own.
- Dual-route and word-recognition models help describe how printed words may be recognized or read aloud, but they do not define the developmental relationship among signed-language access, signed-language literacy, and durable signed text.
- Language-deprivation and critical-period research help explain why early accessible language matters, but they do not by themselves describe what changes when direct signed-language literacy becomes available later.

The model's contribution is therefore not a replacement for those frameworks.

It is a sorting model for the signed-language writing problem:

> separate developmental language pathways from durable literacy pathways, then ask which pathways actually exist for the learner and the community.

More formally, the four-pathway model is a research-organizing model rather than a replacement for acquisition theory, reading theory, or neurocognitive models of language.

Its purpose is to sort the signed-language writing problem into distinct access conditions:

- language input
- language output
- literacy input
- literacy output

The model is useful if it helps researchers state which pathway is present, constrained, delayed, or missing, and what kind of evidence would be needed to study that condition.

## Language comes first

The first major developmental issue is not reading.

It is language.

A child develops language when linguistic input is accessible, socially meaningful, and repeatedly available, and when the child also has usable expressive routes back out into interaction.

This point is easy to lose when literacy is culturally overvalued or when speech is treated as the default model of language.

But developmentally, language and literacy are not coequal starting points.

Language is primary.

Literacy builds later on top of it.

This is true whether the early language route is:

- auditory and spoken
- visual and signed
- tactile in forms of tactile language access

The crucial issue is not which modality is socially dominant.

The crucial issue is whether the learner has real access to language.

That requires distinguishing two questions that are often collapsed:

- what language is present in the environment
- what language is actually accessible to the learner

Those are not the same thing.

A language can be socially available without being stably accessible.

That difference matters because the developmental costs of limited or unstable access emerge before literacy can plausibly compensate.

## Literacy comes second

Literacy should be treated as a second developmental layer.

It does not create language from nothing.

It adds durable symbolic routes to an already linguistic system.

Those routes matter because they allow language to be:

- revisited
- revised
- compared
- indexed
- searched
- shared across time and distance

This is why literacy is more than storage.

It changes what language users can do with language.

It also changes what teachers, researchers, and institutions can do with language.

For the purposes of this paper, the key point is that literacy adds durable symbolic input and output.

That addition should not be confused with early language formation itself.

Language first.

Literacy second.

The second depends on the first more than the first depends on the second.

## The four pathways

The model proposed here distinguishes four pathways:

1. **language input pathways**  
How language reaches the learner as language.

2. **language output pathways**  
How the learner expresses language back into interaction.

3. **literacy input pathways**  
How language is received as durable symbolic text.

4. **literacy output pathways**  
How language is authored as durable symbolic text.

The point of the model is not to produce a neat quadrant for its own sake.

The point is to clarify that early language access and later literacy access are related, but not identical, developmental conditions.

### Language input

Language input pathways are primary because they nourish the formation of a language system.

Here again, *pathway* is being used in the access-and-development sense defined above, not as a claim that each pathway is a discrete neural circuit.

Examples include:

- auditory speech for many hearing children
- visual signed language for many Deaf children with early sign access
- tactile language access in DeafBlind contexts

These routes are not interchangeable in practice, but they do the same kind of developmental work: they make language available to the learner as language.

### Language output

Language output pathways matter because language development is not only receptive.

Children need ways to express language into the world, test it in interaction, and participate socially in it.

Examples include:

- speaking
- signing
- tactile expressive systems in DeafBlind communication

Output matters because language grows through use, not only through passive reception.

### Literacy input

Literacy input pathways are later-arising durable symbolic routes.

Examples include:

- print reading
- Braille reading
- potentially SignWriting reading

What unites them is not modality but durability and symbolic structure.

### Literacy output

Literacy output pathways allow the learner to author durable symbolic language.

Examples include:

- handwriting
- typing
- Braille writing
- potentially SignWriting composition

These pathways complete the literacy layer by making written language not only readable but writable.

## Model statement and testable expectations

The model can be stated compactly:

> language development depends first on accessible language input and usable language output; literacy adds later durable symbolic input and output pathways that can amplify, preserve, and reorganize language use once language exists.

For signed languages, the key research consequence is:

> direct signed-language writing adds literacy input and literacy output pathways inside the signed language rather than only through video, gloss, transcription, or a surrounding spoken-language print system.

Several expectations follow.

### 1. Early language access should matter more than sensory channel

Learners with early accessible signed language should be expected to build linguistic foundations even without auditory speech access.

Learners with delayed or unstable first-language access should face constrained developmental opportunity regardless of whether later literacy instruction is strong.

### 2. Direct signed-language literacy should create different text-use affordances

If SignWriting functions as literacy input and literacy output, readers and writers should be able to use it for tasks that video and gloss do not support as directly:

- rereading
- revision
- annotation
- comparison
- citation
- corpus search
- classroom reuse

### 3. Literacy output should matter, not only literacy input

A learner who can read signed text but cannot easily author it has only part of the literacy layer.

Writing, typing, editing, and revising should therefore be studied as part of the model rather than treated as secondary after reading.

### 4. The model should be revised if durable signed text does not change use

The model would be weakened if direct signed-language text did not produce distinctive affordances over video, gloss, or surrounding-language print in tasks where durability should matter.

Those tasks include annotation, revision, search, comparison, reuse, citation, classroom return, and corpus study, especially when compared with video-only or gloss-only conditions.

It would also need revision if reader experience, classroom use, corpus work, and recognition studies failed to show learnable regularities in signed text.

That is why this paper is a model paper, not a proof that every empirical question is settled.

## Pathway interactions

The four pathways should not be imagined as sealed compartments.

They interact.

Language input and language output form the core developmental exchange through which language is built and exercised.

Literacy input and literacy output later intersect with that system by making language durable and symbolically manipulable.

That means the model is developmental, not merely taxonomic.

It is meant to explain order and dependence:

- language pathways are foundational
- literacy pathways are later augmentations
- literacy pathways can amplify language use once language exists

## Pathway constraints and developmental cost

This model becomes useful because it clarifies what it means for pathways to be constrained.

The most careful statement is not that every constrained pathway produces the same outcome or that the brain simply “stagnates” in a literal sense.

The more defensible claim is that reduced, delayed, unstable, or inaccessible pathways constrain developmental opportunity.

That matters especially for the language layer.

If language input is delayed or partial, then later literacy has less stable structure to build on.

If expressive output is constrained, then the learner has less opportunity to participate in reciprocal language use.

If literacy pathways are missing, then language may still be fully linguistic, but it lacks durable symbolic routes that support revision, comparison, textual accumulation, and broader forms of educational and research reuse.

Different pathway constraints have different effects.

The model's central claim is that pathway configuration matters, that access matters, and that developmental timing matters.

## Signed-language writing as a new literacy condition

This is where signed-language writing becomes more than an abstract add-on.

If a signed language gains durable readable and writable text of its own, then the pathway landscape changes.

What becomes possible is not only better notation or better archiving.

A new literacy condition becomes available:

- literacy input within the signed language itself
- literacy output within the signed language itself

This matters because video, surrounding spoken-language literacy, and research transcription systems do not all do the same job.

Video preserves performance, but it is not the same as durable text.

Literacy in a surrounding spoken language can be powerful and necessary, but it is not the same as native signed-language literacy.

Research transcription systems can be analytically useful, but they are not always designed for ordinary readable and writable use.

The question, then, is not whether signed people can use video or bilingual literacy.

The question is what changes when signed language itself has durable symbolic pathways.

That is the condition under which SignWriting becomes scientifically meaningful.

## What this model establishes

The model claims:

- language development and literacy development should be distinguished
- accessible pathways matter more than loyalty to one sensory channel
- literacy is a second developmental layer
- signed-language writing changes the literacy landscape in principle
- that changed landscape opens real research questions

The model is a research framework, not an outcome claim.

Its value is that it separates questions that are often collapsed: early language access, later literacy, input, output, signed-language writing, reading, syntax, and educational outcomes.

Those distinctions make future empirical work easier to design and easier to interpret.

The usefulness of the model lies partly in its ability to define a research space without pretending that the research space has already been exhausted.

## Research questions that follow

Once the four pathways are separated, a more disciplined research program becomes possible.

### 1. Development and access

- What kinds of early pathway configurations best support strong language development?
- How should language access and literacy access be distinguished in educational design?
- What are the consequences of direct signed-language literacy pathways becoming available later than language pathways?

### 2. Reading and recognition

- What does reading mean in a plane-based signed writing system?
- How do readers resolve structure, similarity, and spatial relation?
- What kinds of near-neighbor competition emerge in signed text?

### 3. Syntax and spatial reference

- How are spatial relations, anaphora, and structured reference carried into written form?
- What does syntactic organization look like when writing is not modeled only on a line?

### 4. Education and institutional use

- How do learners use written signed language in real educational settings?
- What changes when text, rather than video alone, becomes part of classroom practice?

### 5. Corpora and reusable infrastructure

- What texts should be collected?
- What annotation and search layers are needed?
- How can studies become cumulative rather than isolated?

These questions are not all answered here.

They are made clearer here.

## Why this paper belongs after Language Without Sound

*Language Without Sound* was right to insist that language is not reducible to sound.

But once that conceptual ground is cleared, the next task is not to repeat the claim louder.

The next task is to model the developmental and literacy consequences more explicitly.

That is what this paper is for.

It should therefore be read as a second move:

- not a replacement for the foundations essay
- not a compressed infrastructure pitch
- but the first real anchor of the research-facing series

## Conclusion

The four-pathway model is a way of clarifying the developmental order of the problem.

Language comes first.

Literacy comes second.

Both depend on accessible pathways, but they do not depend on the same kinds of pathways in the same way.

Once that distinction is made, signed-language writing becomes easier to understand.

It is not merely a novel notation.

It is the possibility of adding durable literacy pathways to a language that has too often been forced to rely on performance, video, or surrounding-language text for functions that durable native text could support more directly.

That does not settle the science.

It gives the science a clearer model from which to begin.

From here, the strongest next move is *Research Program for Literacy Without Sound*, which carries the model into study design, dataset priorities, and reusable research infrastructure.
